Gen Z Digital natives

The new generation of children, known as digital natives, are growing up using digital technologies from their childhood. Outside the classroom, their world is flooded with a variety of content. Everything is always at your fingertips. Anything can be learned. Learn from the best teachers in the world to teach it in every subject. You can study as deeply as you like. Online content, not printed textbooks. Some are interactive, and there's competition for better layouts. It is a competitive field to present the best ideas in the shortest possible time. When they sit in the classroom they feel a downgrade from all of this.

Gen Zers are “digital natives,” a term coined by writer and speaker Marc Prensky. He originally used the term to refer to millennials born after 1980, as they were the first to grow up with connected computers. But Gen Z takes this term to a new level. Born in 1996 and later, these students grew up not only with computers and internet access, but also with smartphones, social media, and mobile devices.

Digital Natives Digital Immigrants.(pdf) Marc Prensky, October 2001.

A few college professors recently shared these kinds of problems with me. That the children sit in a class of 45 minutes or an hour and are very worried. Our average attention span is 20 minutes.

Microsoft Attention Spans Research Report - Microsoft Canada

But why we get so bored so fast? It seems to be all driven by dopamine, the chemical released every time we do something rewarding and that makes us feel good. Being used to Internet, social networks and instant messaging, we got accustomed to constantly get instant gratification when we find something interesting, spiking dopamine in our brain.

This is why, when we perceive what we are reading or watching as dull or pointless, our attention span tends to be dramatically reduced: we simply don’t get our dopamine hit by non-engaging or non-interesting contents, feeling the opposite of satisfied and happy.

... If people, both digital natives and immigrants, are so used to being involved in interactive activities and receiving instant gratification, that’s exactly what they should get while learning something: we cannot expect to use old methods to engage students who have a new way of thinking. Therefore, training needs to be tailored to meet these new behaviors, “borrowing” structures and features from those digital tools we all use in our everyday life. - Digital natives: what has really changed?

We Need to Rethink Education for Digital Natives- Adobe Communications Team

These digital natives are fast-paced, visually-oriented, nonlinear, always-on learners. They have a built-in instinct to swipe right when reading books, and the pace of technological change doesn’t phase them. It’s no wonder the old rules of engagement don’t resonate with them, and, more importantly, aren’t preparing them to thrive in an unknown future.

“The role of a teacher is fundamentally changing,” says Richard Culatta, chief executive officer of the International Society for Technology in Education (ISTE). “In the past, teachers were the content providers because they were the only really reliable, ubiquitous form of delivering instruction. That’s just not the case anymore.”

Twentieth-century teaching methods and 21st-century technology represent a generation gap like no other, and that disconnect is coming head-to-head in the classroom. Gen Z isn’t interested in traditional passive learning. More than half say they learn best by doing, while only one in 10 say they learn best by listening. The students’ results make it clear: failure rates for lecture classes are 55 percent higher than active-learning environments. - Getting to Know Gen Z(PDF)

Embedded ICT

Embedded ICT is the use of digital technologies in the teaching of all subjects. It is not a separate subject but a way of teaching all subjects. This model is effective and pragmatic. I have observed this model in the ICT curriculum of the Kerala. Along with the digitally equipped classrooms, the teachers are trained to use digital technologies in their teaching. Using the computer, projector, and internet, the teachers can show the students the best resources available on the internet. The teachers can also create their own resources and share them with the students.

How to Integrate Technology - Nana Gilbertson

There are some struggles in this model. The first is the lack of resources, especially in non-English languages. It require time to prepare the resources even though, internet has plenty of these resouces. The teachers need to be trained to use the digital technologies. However just showing visual content or or some presentation on the screen does not mean they are engaging the students.

An alternative approach for interactive content is to author content directly for the web instead of using elearning platforms. While this typically requires content authors to have a deeper technical knowledge, it offers much more flexibility. We can create a much wider variety of types of experiences and above all, it is easy to publicly publish web content and share it with others.

Striking the Right Balance

In our increasingly digital world, the importance of computer science education cannot be overstated. However, as we integrate this crucial subject into school curricula, we must be mindful of maintaining a proportional approach that preserves the balance among all fundamental subjects taught in schools.

Two related essays I wrote recently:
  1. Concerns on using AI in classrooms
  2. Teaching AI in Schools

Computer science undoubtedly offers valuable skills for the 21st century, including logical thinking, problem-solving, and digital literacy. These competencies are essential for many future careers and for navigating our technology-driven society. Yet, we must resist the temptation to overemphasize computer science at the expense of other vital disciplines. Recently, we are seeing the push to introduce artificial intelligence in to the curriculum. As a topic, AI is important in the current world. But we should be careful not to overemphasize it at the expense of other subjects.

Engineers from Malayalam cinema - Times of India

A well-rounded education is the cornerstone of developing critical thinking, creativity, and adaptability. The arts foster creativity and emotional intelligence; history provides context and critical analysis skills; literature enhances communication and empathy; and traditional sciences offer a fundamental understanding of our physical world. Each subject contributes uniquely to a student's cognitive and personal development. Moreover, the interdisciplinary nature of real-world challenges requires a broad knowledge base. Future innovators will need to combine computer science with other fields to solve complex problems. For instance, addressing climate change requires an understanding of environmental science, policy, and technology. Similarly, advancements in healthcare often arise from the intersection of biology, chemistry, and data science. By maintaining a balanced curriculum, we also ensure that students have the opportunity to explore diverse interests and discover their passions. Not every student will pursue a career in technology, and it's crucial to provide a foundation that supports various career paths and life choices.

ICT as Independent course

In today's digital age, introducing fundamental computer science concepts in schools has become essential. A well-designed curriculum should cover a range of topics that provide students with a comprehensive understanding of technology and its implications.

One crucial area is basic user skills. Students should learn how to effectively use computers, navigate operating systems, and utilize common software applications. These skills form the foundation for digital literacy and are vital for academic and professional success. Privacy and security are paramount in our interconnected world. Students need to understand the importance of protecting personal information, recognizing potential online threats, and practicing safe internet habits.

Operating system concepts provide insight into how computers function at a fundamental level. Understanding file systems, process management, and user interfaces gives students a deeper appreciation of the technology they use daily.

Basic programming skills are increasingly valuable across various fields. Introducing students to coding languages like Python or Scratch can develop logical thinking, problem-solving abilities, and creativity. Even if students don't pursue programming careers, the computational thinking skills gained are widely applicable.

Digital literay - Media Literacy

Media literacy is the ability to access, analyze, evaluate, and create media in a variety of forms. It is a critical skill for students in the digital age, enabling them to navigate the vast amount of information available online and make informed decisions about what to believe and share. Media literacy encompasses a range of competencies, including: identifying fake news, understanding bias and propaganda, recognizing persuasive techniques, and evaluating the credibility of sources. By teaching media literacy, educators empower students to be discerning consumers and creators of media content.

News literacy is the ability to determine the credibility of news and other information and to recognize the standards of fact-based journalism to know what to trust, share and act on. https://newslit.org has resources for news literacy.

Stanford University hosts the Civic Online Reasoning website, dedicated to helping students evaluate online information. The COR curriculum is available for free online, and it includes skills such as Teaching Lateral Reading—helping students go beyond what’s posted online by seeing what other information is available about the source. Other topics include how to find better sources of online information, “click restraint,” and more.

Digital Textbooks

A scanned version or PDF version of a textbook is not digital textbook. Atleast that is not my definition of digital textbook. Something boring on paper is still boring on a tablet or a laptop.

“Using technology simply for the sake of using it is wasteful,” Delzer says. “If tech doesn’t transform your classroom, your teaching or your students’ learning, skip it.” - 7 smart ways to use technology in classrooms - Kayla Delzer"

Interactive Linear Algebra - Dan Margalit, Joseph Rabinoff is an example of mixing traditional textbook with interactive elements. A printable version is available, but the online version enhances the learning experience.

A digital textbook is a textbook that is designed to be read on a computer, tablet, or smartphone. It is interactive, multimedia-rich, and can be updated easily. It can include videos, animations, simulations, and other interactive elements. It can be accessed online or downloaded for offline use. It can be personalized to the individual student, with adaptive learning features that adjust to the student's pace and level of understanding. It will have hyperlinks and references to other resources for further study.
By opening up the textbook to the web, we can make it a living document that is always up-to-date with the latest information. It won't be limited to students, but for anyone interested in the subject.

One of the best examples of Web's capability in interactive education: Earth and Sun by Bartosz Ciechanowski. Bartosz Ciechanowski is known for his interactive visualizations that explains the concepts in the most intuitive way. Do checkout his website.
The Radical Transformation of the Textbook - wired.com

Accessible education

Another advantage of digital textbooks is that they are more accessible to students with disabilities. They can be read aloud by screen readers, enlarged for students with visual impairments, and navigated by students with mobility impairments.

Accessible Textbooks for All is a project that aims to make textbooks accessible to students with disabilities.

Availability of educational content in many languages is a challenge. For example, in the age of AI, when we look at the corpus in Malayalam language in the web, it is mostly religious, entertainment or political content. There is very little educational content. The same is true for many other languages.

10 years ago, Volunteers at Swathanthra Malayalam Computing, including myself, worked with Kerala State's education department to publish textbooks in unicode, and make it available in online in variable formats, including braille. However, our efforts were not successful.


In a major boost to the free software movement in the state, the school curriculum steering committee has decided to set school textbooks in universally-accessible Unicode format. Now, free software to set school textbooks in Kerala - Times of India, Nov, 2013

I would love to see a transformation from printed textbooks to online interactive textbooks. This transformation should be iterative and should complement the existing printed books(it is not going away soon). I would love to see videos, hyperlinks, animations, illustration, hyperlinks, references in it. And students experience the same knowledge seeking journey they are already familiar with in class rooms too.
