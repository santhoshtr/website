Gen Z Digital natives

The current generation of children, often referred to as "digital natives," are immersed in technology from an early age. Beyond the confines of the classroom, they inhabit a world inundated with diverse content, where information is instantly accessible. This digital landscape offers unprecedented learning opportunities, allowing children to engage with expert educators across various disciplines and explore subjects to any desired depth.

Gen Zers are “digital natives,” a term coined by writer and speaker Marc Prensky. He originally used the term to refer to millennials born after 1980, as they were the first to grow up with connected computers. But Gen Z takes this term to a new level. Born in 1996 and later, these students grew up not only with computers and internet access, but also with smartphones, social media, and mobile devices.

Digital Natives Digital Immigrants.(pdf) Marc Prensky, October 2001.

In contrast to traditional printed textbooks, online resources provide interactive and visually appealing content. Content creators compete to present complex ideas concisely and engagingly, constantly innovating to capture and maintain attention. This dynamic digital environment has set a high standard for information delivery and consumption.

Consequently, when these digital natives enter the conventional classroom setting, they may experience a sense of disconnection or even regression. The stark contrast between the rich, interactive digital world they're accustomed to and the often more static, traditional learning environment can leave them feeling understimulated and disengaged.

A few college professors recently shared these kinds of problems with me. That the children sit in a class of 45 minutes or an hour and are very worried. Our average attention span is 20 minutes.

Microsoft Attention Spans Research Report - Microsoft Canada

But why we get so bored so fast? It seems to be all driven by dopamine, the chemical released every time we do something rewarding and that makes us feel good. Being used to Internet, social networks and instant messaging, we got accustomed to constantly get instant gratification when we find something interesting, spiking dopamine in our brain.

This is why, when we perceive what we are reading or watching as dull or pointless, our attention span tends to be dramatically reduced: we simply don’t get our dopamine hit by non-engaging or non-interesting contents, feeling the opposite of satisfied and happy.

... If people, both digital natives and immigrants, are so used to being involved in interactive activities and receiving instant gratification, that’s exactly what they should get while learning something: we cannot expect to use old methods to engage students who have a new way of thinking. Therefore, training needs to be tailored to meet these new behaviors, “borrowing” structures and features from those digital tools we all use in our everyday life. - Digital natives: what has really changed?

We Need to Rethink Education for Digital Natives- Adobe Communications Team

These digital natives are fast-paced, visually-oriented, nonlinear, always-on learners. They have a built-in instinct to swipe right when reading books, and the pace of technological change doesn’t phase them. It’s no wonder the old rules of engagement don’t resonate with them, and, more importantly, aren’t preparing them to thrive in an unknown future.

“The role of a teacher is fundamentally changing,” says Richard Culatta, chief executive officer of the International Society for Technology in Education (ISTE). “In the past, teachers were the content providers because they were the only really reliable, ubiquitous form of delivering instruction. That’s just not the case anymore.”

Twentieth-century teaching methods and 21st-century technology represent a generation gap like no other, and that disconnect is coming head-to-head in the classroom. Gen Z isn’t interested in traditional passive learning. More than half say they learn best by doing, while only one in 10 say they learn best by listening. The students’ results make it clear: failure rates for lecture classes are 55 percent higher than active-learning environments. - Getting to Know Gen Z(PDF)

Embedded ICT

Embedded ICT refers to the seamless integration of digital technologies across all subject areas, rather than treating it as a separate discipline. This approach, both effective and pragmatic, has been successfully implemented in Kerala's ICT curriculum. In this model, classrooms are equipped with digital tools, and teachers are trained to incorporate technology into their instructional methods.

How to Integrate Technology - Nana Gilbertson

By utilizing computers, projectors, and internet connectivity, educators can expose students to high-quality online resources. Additionally, teachers have the opportunity to create and disseminate their own digital materials.

However, there are challenges to overcome. One significant issue is the scarcity of resources, particularly in languages other than English. Despite the abundance of online materials, curating and adapting them for specific needs can be time-consuming. Furthermore, comprehensive teacher training in digital technology usage is essential. It's important to note that merely displaying visual content or presentations on a screen does not guarantee student engagement. True integration requires thoughtful implementation and interactive elements.

An alternative strategy involves creating web-based content directly, bypassing traditional e-learning platforms. While this approach demands greater technical expertise from content creators, it offers enhanced flexibility. This method allows for a broader range of interactive experiences and facilitates easier public sharing and distribution of educational materials. This web-centric approach enables educators to craft more diverse and engaging learning experiences, potentially revolutionizing the way educational content is developed and disseminated.

While choosing the technology, tools and applications for this kind of practice, I would strongly urge to based it on free and opensource software. Software used in school education should respect the privacy, security aspects.

Striking the Right Balance

In our increasingly digital world, the importance of computer science education cannot be overstated. However, as we integrate this crucial subject into school curricula, we must be mindful of maintaining a proportional approach that preserves the balance among all fundamental subjects taught in schools.

Computer science undoubtedly offers valuable skills for the 21st century, including logical thinking, problem-solving, and digital literacy. These competencies are essential for many future careers and for navigating our technology-driven society. Yet, we must resist the temptation to overemphasize computer science at the expense of other vital disciplines. Recently, we are seeing the push to introduce artificial intelligence in to the curriculum. As a topic, AI is important in the current world. But we should be careful not to overemphasize it at the expense of other subjects.

A well-rounded education is the cornerstone of developing critical thinking, creativity, and adaptability. The arts foster creativity and emotional intelligence; history provides context and critical analysis skills; literature enhances communication and empathy; and traditional sciences offer a fundamental understanding of our physical world. Each subject contributes uniquely to a student's cognitive and personal development. Moreover, the interdisciplinary nature of real-world challenges requires a broad knowledge base. Future innovators will need to combine computer science with other fields to solve complex problems. For instance, addressing climate change requires an understanding of environmental science, policy, and technology. Similarly, advancements in healthcare often arise from the intersection of biology, chemistry, and data science. By maintaining a balanced curriculum, we also ensure that students have the opportunity to explore diverse interests and discover their passions. Not every student will pursue a career in technology, and it's crucial to provide a foundation that supports various career paths and life choices.

ICT as Independent course

In the contemporary digital landscape, integrating fundamental computer science concepts into school curricula has become imperative. A well-structured educational framework should encompass a diverse range of topics, equipping students with a comprehensive understanding of technology and its far-reaching implications.

Key components of such a curriculum include:

  1. Essential Digital Literacy: Students should develop proficiency in basic computer operations, including navigating operating systems and utilizing common software applications. These foundational skills are crucial for both academic success and future professional endeavors.
  2. Privacy and Cybersecurity Awareness: In our interconnected world, understanding the importance of data protection is paramount. Students must learn to safeguard personal information, identify potential online threats, and cultivate safe internet practices.
  3. Operating System Fundamentals: Providing insight into the core functioning of computers, this knowledge area covers file systems, process management, and user interfaces. Such understanding fosters a deeper appreciation for the technology that permeates daily life.
  4. Introduction to Programming: Basic coding skills are increasingly valuable across various disciplines. Exposure to programming languages like Python or Scratch can enhance logical thinking, problem-solving capabilities, and creativity. The computational thinking skills developed through coding are widely applicable, even for students who don't pursue programming careers.

By incorporating these elements, educational institutions can prepare students for the digital future, fostering a generation of tech-savvy, security-conscious, and analytically minded individuals. This approach not only enhances digital competence but also cultivates critical thinking skills essential for navigating the complexities of our technology-driven world.

Digital literay - Media Literacy

Media literacy is the ability to access, analyze, evaluate, and create media in a variety of forms. It is a critical skill for students in the digital age, enabling them to navigate the vast amount of information available online and make informed decisions about what to believe and share. Media literacy encompasses a range of competencies, including: identifying fake news, understanding bias and propaganda, recognizing persuasive techniques, and evaluating the credibility of sources. By teaching media literacy, educators empower students to be discerning consumers and creators of media content.

News literacy is the ability to determine the credibility of news and other information and to recognize the standards of fact-based journalism to know what to trust, share and act on. https://newslit.org has resources for news literacy.

Stanford University hosts the Civic Online Reasoning website, dedicated to helping students evaluate online information. The COR curriculum is available for free online, and it includes skills such as Teaching Lateral Reading—helping students go beyond what’s posted online by seeing what other information is available about the source. Other topics include how to find better sources of online information, “click restraint,” and more.

Digital Textbooks

A scanned version or PDF version of a textbook is not digital textbook. Atleast that is not my definition of digital textbook. Something boring on paper is still boring on a tablet or a laptop.

“Using technology simply for the sake of using it is wasteful,” Delzer says. “If tech doesn’t transform your classroom, your teaching or your students’ learning, skip it.” - 7 smart ways to use technology in classrooms - Kayla Delzer"

Interactive Linear Algebra - Dan Margalit, Joseph Rabinoff is an example of mixing traditional textbook with interactive elements. A printable version is available, but the online version enhances the learning experience.

A digital textbook is an innovative educational resource designed for consumption on various electronic devices, including computers, tablets, and smartphones. These advanced learning tools offer a rich, interactive experience that transcends the limitations of traditional print media.

Key features of digital textbooks include:

  1. Multimedia Integration: Incorporation of videos, animations, and simulations to enhance understanding and engagement.
  2. Interactivity: Dynamic elements that allow students to actively participate in the learning process.
  3. Accessibility: Available both online and offline, catering to diverse learning environments and preferences.
  4. Personalization: Adaptive learning features that tailor content to individual student needs, adjusting pace and difficulty based on comprehension levels.
  5. Expandability: Hyperlinks and references to external resources, facilitating deeper exploration of topics.
  6. Updateability: Easy modification to ensure content remains current and relevant.

By leveraging web connectivity, digital textbooks evolve into living documents, constantly refreshed with the latest information. This approach not only benefits students but also extends learning opportunities to anyone interested in the subject matter, regardless of formal educational status.

The dynamic nature of digital textbooks represents a significant shift in educational resources, offering a more flexible, comprehensive, and accessible approach to learning that aligns with the needs of the modern, digitally-oriented learner.

Accessible education

Another advantage of digital textbooks is that they are more accessible to students with disabilities. They can be read aloud by screen readers, enlarged for students with visual impairments, and navigated by students with mobility impairments.

Accessible Textbooks for All is a project that aims to make textbooks accessible to students with disabilities.

Availability of educational content in many languages is a challenge. For example, in the age of AI, when we look at the corpus in Malayalam language in the web, it is mostly religious, entertainment or political content. There is very little educational content. The same is true for many other languages.

10 years ago, Volunteers at Swathanthra Malayalam Computing, including myself, worked with Kerala State's education department to publish textbooks in unicode, and make it available in online in variable formats, including braille. However, our efforts were not successful.


In a major boost to the free software movement in the state, the school curriculum steering committee has decided to set school textbooks in universally-accessible Unicode format. Now, free software to set school textbooks in Kerala - Times of India, Nov, 2013

The evolution from traditional printed textbooks to interactive online resources is a compelling prospect for modern education. This transition should be viewed as a gradual, iterative process that complements, rather than immediately replaces, existing print materials. The enduring presence of physical books in the near future necessitates a harmonious coexistence between traditional and digital formats.

An ideal digital textbook would incorporate a rich array of multimedia elements, including:

  1. Embedded videos for visual explanations
  2. Interactive animations to illustrate complex concepts
  3. Dynamic illustrations that respond to user input
  4. Hyperlinks for easy navigation and cross-referencing
  5. Extensive references to facilitate further research

The ultimate goal of this transformation is to recreate and enhance the knowledge-seeking journey that students are accustomed to in traditional classroom settings. By doing so, digital textbooks can provide a seamless bridge between conventional learning methods and the vast potential of interactive, online education.

This approach aims to maintain the familiar structure and depth of traditional textbooks while leveraging digital capabilities to create a more engaging, comprehensive, and adaptable learning experience. It represents a fusion of time-tested educational practices with cutting-edge technology, potentially revolutionizing how students interact with and absorb educational content.

Public education and Edutech

Despite the recent challenges faced by Byju's, the edtech sector remains a fertile ground for innovation and new startups. The industry's potential continues to attract entrepreneurs, driven by several key factors:

  1. Perceived weaknesses in public education: Many edtech companies build their marketing strategies around addressing the shortcomings of traditional public education systems.
  2. Digital education emphasis: The majority of these startups focus on leveraging digital technologies to enhance learning experiences, appealing to an increasingly tech-savvy student population.
  3. Untapped market potential: The education technology domain represents a significant, yet not fully exploited, economic opportunity.
  4. Aspirational middle class: There's a strong desire among middle-class families to provide their children with the best possible educational resources, often viewing edtech solutions as a means to achieve this goal.

In light of these trends, it's crucial for public education systems to recognize and proactively address the challenges posed by the growing edtech sector. Effective ICT (Information and Communication Technology) planning in public education should aim to:

  1. Incorporate innovative digital learning tools and methodologies
  2. Enhance the overall quality and accessibility of education
  3. Bridge the perceived gap between traditional and digital learning approaches

By doing so, public education systems can better compete with private edtech offerings while ensuring that all students, regardless of socioeconomic background, have access to high-quality, technology-enhanced education. This approach would not only improve educational outcomes but also help maintain the relevance and competitiveness of public education in an increasingly digital world.
